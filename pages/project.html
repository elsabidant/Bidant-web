Popcorn and prophecy

Introduction

Fictional narratives have long been debated as serving either a cognitive function, helping individuals simulate possible futures and rehearse responses to uncertain situations, or a purely entertainment-driven function, relying on exaggerated cues that humans are already predisposed to enjoy, such as threat, dominance, romance, or technological power. This opposition is commonly framed as a contrast between the simulation hypothesis and the entertainment hypothesis.

A key implication of this debate is the possibility of a structural trade-off between predictive accuracy and entertainment value. Narratives that aim to anticipate the future accurately must remain constrained by plausible mechanisms and known regularities, which may limit the exaggeration, surprise, and emotional intensity that often drive audience engagement. Conversely, stories that depart from realism can amplify rewarding stimuli, functioning as superstimuli that enhance enjoyment but reduce predictive fidelity.

Science-fiction cinema provides a privileged testing ground for this question, as it explicitly projects future technologies, social transformations, and global threats. This study investigates whether science-fiction films that more accurately anticipate real future developments are less commercially successful, as predicted by the entertainment hypothesis, or whether anticipatory accuracy instead contributes positively to cultural and economic success.

Hypotheses & prediction

Prediction : Works that predict the future accurately have less cultural success.

  • H0 The accuracy in predicting the future does not influence the popularity of a film.
  • H1 The accuracy in predicting the future negatively influences the popularity of a film.

Material

The study relies on the IMDb dataset of movies released between 1931 and 2019. The initial sample includes 1,455 science-fiction films with basic metadata (title, plot, year of release, genre, language, worldwide gross income, etc.).

For the analysis, we restrict the sample to films with a non-missing worldwide box office, resulting in N = 588 movies. In addition, we merge this dataset with the IMDb Movies Dataset from Kaggle to obtain budget information, yielding a final analytical sample of N = 360 films.

Annotation method

We rely on an automatic annotation procedure powered by GPT-5.2 to compute an holistic measure of anticipatory accuracy. GPT is asked directly how much a past film anticipates the present, yielding a continuous score from 0 to 5.

Prompt

Rate how much this past movie anticipates the present by giving a score from 0 to 5, where
0 = no resemblance to later real developments;
1 = extremely vague or coincidental resemblance;
2 = limited partial anticipation with few identifiable parallels;
3 = moderate anticipation with several partial or general parallels;
4 = strong anticipation with multiple meaningful and relatively precise correspondences;
5 = exceptional anticipation with numerous specific, accurate, and well-defined parallels to later real-world developments.
Base the rating only on elements shown in the movie that later occurred in reality, justify briefly in one paragraph, and ignore author intentions.
Then finish your message in the following format: Score =
The film is :


An alternative annotation procedure was implemented at an early stage of the project, relying on a claim-based strategy (see Devlog). The anticipatory accuracy scores derived from this procedure converged with those obtained using the current annotation protocol, suggesting that the measurement is robust to reasonable variations in annotation strategy.

Analysis method

To test our hypothesis, we compute a multivariate linear regression that models the cultural success (approximated by the worldwide gross income) based on the year and the accuracy of future prediction.
The regression uses the following formula :

log(Box officei) = β0 + β1·Accuracyi + β2·log(Budget)i + β3·Yeari + εi

We focus on a one-sided (left-tailed) test on β1, asking whether higher anticipation accuracy is associated with lower box-office revenue.

Results

The main coefficient of interest, anticipatory accuracy, shows a positive and statistically significant association with box-office performance (β1 = 0.213). The conventional two-sided p-value for this effect is p < 0.001. However, the left-tailed p-value is p = 0.999. This result provides no support for the pre-registered directional hypothesis that more accurate films would be less commercially successful.

By contrast, the right-tailed p-value is p < 0.001, indicating strong evidence in favor of a positive relationship between anticipatory accuracy and box-office revenue.

Among the control variables, production budget emerges as a strong and highly significant predictor of box-office revenue (β2 = 0.450, p < 0.001). In contrast, the effect of release year is not statistically significant in this model (β3 = 0.061, p = 0.206), suggesting that temporal trends are largely accounted for by differences in budget and film characteristics.

Overall, the model explains a meaningful share of the variance in box-office performance (R² = 0.241), indicating that approximately 24% of the variation in revenue is accounted for by anticipatory accuracy, production budget, and release year.

Results interpretation

Overall, these findings contradict the pre-registered entertainment hypothesis and instead support the simulation hypothesis: science-fiction films that more accurately anticipate future developments tend to achieve greater commercial success. Rather than indicating a trade-off between realism and popularity, anticipatory accuracy appears to function as a positive contributor to cultural and economic outcomes in this sample.
