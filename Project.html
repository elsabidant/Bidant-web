Anticipation study

Anticipation in fiction

Exploring whether anticipation stories (especially films) truly anticipate future events, and how these visions align with what had really happen since their publication.

Objective

Test whether anticipatory cultural imaginaries (novels, films, comics) actually predict future elements, and analyse how visions of the future evolve across 1800–2100.

Motivation

Near- and far-future fiction sometimes reflects and even anticipates social, technological, and environmental transformations. This project examines how these narratives align with scientific scenarios, current debates, and events that have already occurred.

Expected Results (Notebook)

Temporal views of predictions

Visualise predictions across periods and compare their emphasis over time.

Distance to reality

Measure proximity between anticipatory imaginaries and the real world.

Case studies

Explore focused cases (e.g., climate, technological change).

Target audience: researchers and students in digital humanities interested in linking future imaginaries with scientific and media data.

Key Tasks

  • Collect a corpus of anticipatory works (via Wikipedia & IMDb) with metadata.
  • Write a pre-registration.
  • Use an LLM to detect anticipation types present in works.
  • Use an LLM to estimate how much a work is anchored in its time vs. today.
  • Group works by narrative themes.
  • Test the prediction: anticipatory works do not anticipate the future more than other works.
  • Map works to the real world (by presented events).
  • Prototype visualisation.
  • Draft an article.

MVP

  • A clean dataset of fictional works (literature and cinema) with metadata, their themes, and an “anticipation” score inferred via an LLM.

Resources

  • Data: IMDb, Wikipedia
  • Tools: Python (pandas, numpy, scikit-learn, transformers), GPT
  • Related work: “Quantifying and explaining the rise of fiction” (method inspiration for LLM use on fiction).
