Research project

Popcorn and prophecy

Investigation on whether science-fiction movies truly anticipate future developments or mainly provide compelling entertainment dressed up as prophecy.

Description

Fiction has been argued to either function as a simulation tool, helping us rehearse possible futures, or as pure entertainment that amplifies cues humans already enjoy (threat, dominance, romance, technological power). Science fiction is a privileged ground for this question because it explicitly projects future technologies, social transformations, and environmental crises.

This study focuses on science-fiction cinema and asks whether films that are more accurate in anticipating future states of the world are more or less successful at the box office.

Hypotheses & prediction

Prediction : Works that predict the future accurately have less cultural success.

  • H0 The accuracy in predicting the future does not influence the popularity of a film.
  • H1 The accuracy in predicting the future negatively influences the popularity of a film.

Material

The study relies on the IMDb dataset of science-fiction movies released between 1931 and 2019. The initial sample includes 1,455 science-fiction films with basic metadata (title, plot, year of release, genre, language, worldwide gross income, etc.).

For the analysis, we restrict the sample to films with a non-missing worldwide box office, resulting in N = 588 movies.

Annotation method

We use a Large Language Model (GPT-4) to automatically annotate each film with factual claims about the world it depicts.

Claims extraction
Extract 5 concrete, testable claims about the film’s world.

Analysis method

To test our hypothesis, we compute a multivariate linear regression that models the cultural success (approximated by the worldwide gross income) based on the year and the accuracy of future prediction.
The regression uses the following formula :

log(Box officei) = β0 + β1·Accuracyi + β2·Yeari + εi

We focus on a one-sided (left-tailed) test on β1, asking whether higher anticipation accuracy is associated with lower box-office revenue. The one-tailed p-value is the probability, under the null hypothesis of no effect, of observing a coefficient as negative as ours (or more), so a small value directly supports our directional prediction.
